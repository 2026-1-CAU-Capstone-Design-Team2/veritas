## Summary
This blog post summarizes the state of VLA research at ICLR 2026, highlighting the explosive growth in submissions and key trends. It defines VLA models as those with internet-scale pretraining on vision-language data, distinguishing them from multimodal policies without such training. The author notes that while discrete diffusion and embodied reasoning are growing, they face challenges like slow inference and limited labeled datasets. Practical guidance is provided for interpreting benchmark results, noting that LIBERO and CALVIN benchmarks are often saturated by current SOTA models and do not require extensive pretraining to achieve competitive performance. The post concludes with a discussion on the hidden gap between frontier labs and academic research labs.

## Key Points
- ICLR 2026 saw an 18x increase in VLA submissions compared to 2024, signaling rapid growth in the field.
- The author defines VLA as models with internet-scale pretraining on vision-language data, distinguishing them from multimodal policies lacking such training.
- Discrete diffusion and embodied reasoning are emerging trends but face challenges including slow inference and limited labeled datasets.
- Benchmark results like LIBERO and CALVIN are often saturated by current SOTA models, making it difficult to determine absolute superiority without extensive pretraining.
- There is a significant gap between frontier labs and academic research labs that is not visible from reading papers alone.
- Practitioners should interpret benchmark results carefully, noting that most papers test against each other rather than comparing against state-of-the-art baselines.
